01 / 04

Splitting Atoms

in 1905, Albert Einstein came up with his famous equation E = mc². It basically meant that a tiny piece of matter could turn into a massive amount of energy. But for a really long time, nobody actually knew how to do it in real life.

Then in 1938, some scientists in Germany figured out that if you shoot tiny particles called neutrons at a heavy metal called uranium, the atom splits right in half! A scientist named Lise Meitner called this fission. When the atom splits, it releases energy and even more neutrons.

Those extra neutrons then smash into other uranium atoms, splitting them too. This creates a huge chain reaction. If you get enough of this material together at once (they call it a "critical mass"), it doesn't just get hot. it makes a giant, crazy explosion.

Fig. 2 — A simple chain reaction

"A chain reaction might be set up if neutrons liberated by fission could cause further fissions in neighboring nuclei."

— Leo Szilárd, a smart physicist, 1933
02 / 04

The Manhattan Project

In 1939, right before World War II, Albert Einstein sent a letter to President Roosevelt warning him that Germany might be building an atomic bomb. So, after Pearl Harbor happened, the U.S. started a massive, top-secret mission called the Manhattan Project.

A guy named General Leslie Groves and a scientist named J. Robert Oppenheimer were in charge. They hired over 130,000 people to work in secret towns all over the country. It cost about $2 billion back then, which is like $28 billion today. That is a ton of money!

There were three main secret bases. One in Oak Ridge, Tennessee, one in Hanford, Washington, and a really important lab in Los Alamos, New Mexico, where they actually designed the bombs.

Dec 1942

The First Chain Reaction

A scientist named Enrico Fermi and his team made the first controlled atom-splitting chain reaction under a football stadium in Chicago. This proved the bomb could actually work.

1943–44

The Secret Lab

Oppenheimer got the smartest scientists in the world together in the middle of nowhere to build two types of bombs: one using uranium and one using plutonium.

May 1945

Germany Quits

Germany gave up in May. We didn't need to use the bomb on them anymore, but the project kept going so they could use it on Japan to end the war.

Jul 16, 1945

The Trinity Test

They tested the very first atomic bomb in the New Mexico desert. The explosion was way bigger than anyone thought it would be. Oppenheimer remembered an old quote: "Now I am become Death, the destroyer of worlds." It was pretty scary.

03 / 04

The Two Bombs

The scientists actually made two completely different types of bombs. They both worked differently, but they were both dropped on Japan just three days apart.

Fig. 3 — The "Little Boy" bomb (dropped on Hiroshima)

The first bomb was called "Little Boy". It worked kind of like a gun inside a tube. It shot one piece of uranium into another piece. When they smashed together, it made a critical mass and exploded. It was a simple idea, but uranium was super hard to make.

Fig. 4 — The "Fat Man" bomb (dropped on Nagasaki)

The second bomb was called "Fat Man". This one was way more complicated. It used a ball of plutonium surrounded by regular explosives. When those explosives blew up, they squished the plutonium ball inward super fast, causing a huge nuclear explosion.

04 / 04

What Happened After

The U.S. dropped Little Boy on the city of Hiroshima on August 6, 1945. It instantly killed around 70,000 to 80,000 people. Then, three days later, they dropped Fat Man on Nagasaki and killed about 40,000 more. Japan finally gave up on August 15, and World War II was over.

The people who survived the blasts are called hibakusha in Japanese. They had a really tough time. A lot of them got super sick from radiation and cancer later on. This was the only time nuclear bombs were ever used in a war, and people still argue today about whether it was the right thing to do.

After that, the whole world changed. A few years later, the Soviet Union built their own bomb. This started the Cold War, where the U.S. and the Soviet Union just kept building more and more bombs to scare each other. At one point, they had over 60,000 of them!

Today, there are nine countries that have nuclear weapons. There are rules now trying to stop other countries from getting them. Hiroshima and Nagasaki have big memorials now to remind everyone how terrible these weapons are for regular people, not just in war.

"The release of atomic energy has not created a new problem. It has merely made more urgent the necessity of solving an existing one."
